Chapter I

What is Neurology?

Neurology is the medical specialty and scientific discipline concerned with the structure, function, and disorders of the nervous system. It spans the brain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves, and the intricate signaling pathways that connect them into a unified whole.

At its core, neurology asks fundamental questions: How does a collection of cells generate consciousness? How do electrical impulses become movement, memory, or emotion? What happens when this delicate system is injured, infected, or degenerates?

Neurologists diagnose and treat conditions such as epilepsy, stroke, multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, migraines, and neuropathies. But neurology is far more than clinical medicine, it is the gateway to understanding what makes us thinking, feeling beings.

Chapter II

What are Neurons?

A neuron (also called a nerve cell) is the fundamental building block of the nervous system. Unlike most cells in the body, neurons are highly specialized for one purpose: receiving, processing, and transmitting information through electrical and chemical signals.

Neurons are among the longest-lived cells in the body, many survive your entire lifetime. They are post-mitotic, meaning they do not divide to reproduce. Once damaged beyond repair, the body cannot simply grow a replacement neuron in most regions of the brain.

There are roughly three major structural classes of neurons, each shaped by its function:

  • Multipolar neurons — one axon and many dendrites; the most common type, found in the brain and spinal cord.
  • Bipolar neurons — one axon and one dendrite; found in sensory organs like the retina and inner ear.
  • Unipolar (pseudounipolar) neurons — a single process that splits; common in peripheral sensory nerves.

Anatomy of the Neuron

Dendrites

Tree-like branching extensions that receive incoming signals from other neurons. Their name comes from the Greek dendron (tree). Dendrites are covered in thousands of tiny protrusions called dendritic spines, each hosting a synapse. They conduct graded (analog) electrical signals toward the soma — signals that weaken with distance, unlike the all-or-nothing action potential of the axon.

Soma (Cell Body)

The metabolic hub of the neuron. The soma contains the nucleus and most organelles — mitochondria for energy, rough endoplasmic reticulum for protein synthesis, and Golgi apparatus for packaging neurotransmitters. It integrates all incoming dendritic signals and maintains the cell's structural integrity throughout its lifetime.

Nucleus

The neuron's command center, housing DNA that encodes the proteins essential for neural function — ion channels, receptors, neurotransmitter synthesis enzymes, and structural proteins. Unlike many cells, mature neurons rarely divide, so the nucleus is not actively driving cell division but instead maintaining the specialized gene expression profile that defines neuronal identity.

Axon Hillock

A specialized cone-shaped region where the axon emerges from the soma. This is the neuron's decision point — the site of highest sodium channel density. When the sum of excitatory and inhibitory inputs reaches threshold here, voltage-gated sodium channels open and an action potential is initiated. It is the trigger zone of the neuron.

Axon

A long, slender projection that conducts electrical impulses away from the soma toward other neurons, muscles, or glands. Axons can stretch over a meter in humans (e.g., sciatic nerve fibers). They contain microtubules and neurofilaments for structural support, and transport organelles and vesicles via axonal transport — both anterograde (toward terminals) and retrograde (back to soma).

Myelin Sheath

A fatty insulating layer wrapped around axons by glial cells (oligodendrocytes in the CNS, Schwann cells in the PNS). Myelin is rich in lipids, giving white matter its pale color. It increases conduction velocity by forcing action potentials to saltate (jump) between gaps called nodes of Ranvier, enabling signals to travel up to 120 m/s compared to ~2 m/s in unmyelinated fibers.

Nodes of Ranvier

Tiny unmyelinated gaps between successive myelin segments, named after French pathologist Louis-Antoine Ranvier. These nodes are packed with voltage-gated sodium channels. During saltatory conduction, the action potential effectively "jumps" from node to node, regenerating at each gap. This mechanism makes myelinated nerve conduction both faster and more energy-efficient.

Axon Terminals (Terminal Boutons)

Bulbous endings at the axon's tip where neurotransmitters are stored in synaptic vesicles. When an action potential arrives, voltage-gated calcium channels open, calcium influx triggers vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane, and neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft. A single neuron can form thousands of terminal boutons.

Synapse

The junction between two neurons (or a neuron and an effector cell). The presynaptic terminal releases neurotransmitters that diffuse across the synaptic cleft (~20–40 nm wide) and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. Synapses can be excitatory or inhibitory, and their strength can change over time, the biological basis of learning and memory (synaptic plasticity).

Chapter IV

Glial Cells — The Unsung Architects

Neurons get the spotlight, but glial cells are equally essential

For decades, glial cells (from the Greek glia, meaning "glue") were thought to be mere structural filler. We now know they are active partners in virtually every neural process — from immune defense to synapse formation to myelin production. Glial cells outnumber neurons by roughly 10 to 1, yet each type plays a distinct, indispensable role.

Astrocytes

CNS — star-shaped

The most abundant glial cell in the brain. Astrocytes regulate blood flow, maintain the blood-brain barrier, recycle neurotransmitters, provide metabolic support to neurons, and modulate synaptic activity. Their end-feet wrap around blood vessels and synapses, positioning them as central coordinators of the brain's environment. Reactive astrogliosis occurs after injury.

  • Blood-brain barrier maintenance
  • Potassium and neurotransmitter uptake
  • Glycogen storage and lactate shuttle
  • Synaptic pruning during development

Oligodendrocytes

CNS — myelin producers

Each oligodendrocyte can myelinate multiple axon segments on different neurons — up to 50 separate internodes. Their myelin gives white matter its color and is essential for rapid signal transmission. Destruction of oligodendrocytes causes demyelination, as seen in multiple sclerosis, leading to impaired nerve conduction and neurological deficits.

  • Myelin synthesis and maintenance
  • Axonal metabolic support
  • Saltatory conduction enablement
  • Target of autoimmune attack in MS

Schwann Cells

PNS — peripheral myelination

The peripheral nervous system's equivalent of oligodendrocytes, but each Schwann cell myelinates only one segment of a single axon. They are also critical for peripheral nerve regeneration — Schwann cells form bands of Büngner to guide regrowing axons after injury, a capacity the CNS largely lacks.

  • Peripheral axon myelination
  • Nerve regeneration guidance
  • Phagocytosis of debris after injury
  • Neuromuscular junction maintenance

Microglia

CNS — immune sentinels

The brain's resident immune cells, originating from yolk-sac progenitors (not bone marrow like other immune cells). Microglia constantly survey the CNS with motile processes, phagocytose debris and pathogens, sculpt synapses during development, and drive neuroinflammation in disease. Chronic microglial activation is implicated in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and depression.

  • Pathogen detection and phagocytosis
  • Synaptic pruning and remodeling
  • Inflammatory signaling
  • Surveillance of neural tissue health

Ependymal Cells

CNS — ventricular lining

Ciliated epithelial cells lining the brain's ventricles and central canal of the spinal cord. They help circulate cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which cushions the brain, removes waste products, and distributes nutrients. Some ependymal cells act as neural stem cells in specific niches, contributing to limited neurogenesis in adulthood.

  • Cerebrospinal fluid circulation
  • Brain ventricle lining
  • Neural stem cell niche (subventricular zone)
  • Waste clearance via CSF

Satellite Glial Cells

PNS — ganglia support

Flat cells that surround neuron cell bodies in peripheral ganglia (collections of neuron somas outside the CNS). They regulate the chemical microenvironment around sensory and autonomic neurons, modulate neurotransmitter levels, and may contribute to chronic pain states when dysfunctional, as in some neuropathic pain conditions.

  • Neuronal metabolic support in ganglia
  • Neurotransmitter regulation
  • Structural support for PNS neurons
  • Role in neuropathic pain signaling
Chapter V

Neurological Disorders

From lifelong conditions to temporary setbacks that heal, and how the reversible ones are treated

⚕️

Educational content, not medical advice. Neurological symptoms can signal serious conditions. Always consult a qualified clinician for diagnosis and treatment. Sudden face drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech, a severe "worst-ever" headache, seizures, or loss of consciousness are medical emergencies — seek care immediately.

Neurological conditions span an enormous spectrum. At one end sit chronic and progressive disorders — the nervous system is permanently altered, and care focuses on slowing decline and preserving quality of life. At the other end are temporary, reversible conditions: the nervous system is disrupted but the underlying structure is intact, so with the right therapy most people recover fully.

Chronic Progressive & Long-Term Conditions

These disorders are managed rather than cured. Modern treatment can slow progression, control symptoms, and extend independence, but the changes to the nervous system are lasting.

Alzheimer's Disease

Neurodegenerative

The most common cause of dementia. Amyloid plaques and tau tangles accumulate in the brain, progressively destroying neurons and eroding memory, reasoning, and eventually basic function.

Managed: symptomatic medication, cognitive & lifestyle support.

Parkinson's Disease

Movement Disorder

Loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra causes tremor, rigidity, slowed movement (bradykinesia), and balance problems that worsen over years.

Managed: levodopa, dopamine agonists, deep brain stimulation.

Multiple Sclerosis

Demyelinating · Autoimmune

The immune system attacks the myelin sheath in the CNS, scarring nerve pathways. Symptoms come in relapses — vision loss, numbness, weakness, fatigue — and can accumulate over time.

Managed: disease-modifying therapies to reduce relapses.

Epilepsy

Seizure Disorder

Recurrent seizures caused by bursts of abnormal, synchronized electrical firing in the brain. Severity ranges from brief absences to full convulsions.

Managed: anti-seizure medication — many patients become seizure-free.

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis

Motor Neuron Disease

Progressive degeneration of the motor neurons controlling voluntary muscle. It leads to weakness, muscle wasting, and eventually loss of movement, speech, and breathing.

Managed: supportive care, respiratory & mobility aids.

Huntington's Disease

Genetic · Neurodegenerative

An inherited disorder caused by a CAG-repeat expansion in the HTT gene, producing involuntary movements (chorea), cognitive decline, and psychiatric change in mid-adulthood.

Managed: symptomatic treatment & genetic counseling.

Temporary Reversible Conditions & Their Therapy

These conditions are usually self-limiting or highly treatable. Expand each card to see the specific recovery and therapy approaches — and a typical timeline for getting better.

Tension-Type Headache

Self-limiting

The most common headache — a dull, band-like pressure around the head, often triggered by stress, poor posture, eye strain, or dehydration. Not dangerous and typically resolves on its own.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Rest in a calm, low-stimulation environment and rehydrate.
  • Use over-the-counter analgesics (ibuprofen or acetaminophen) sparingly.
  • Apply a warm compress to the neck and shoulders to release muscle tension.
  • Correct posture and take regular breaks from screens.
  • Practice stress reduction: deep breathing, gentle stretching, and adequate sleep.

Timeline: usually eases within a few hours.

Bell's Palsy

~85% recover

Sudden, one-sided facial weakness or drooping caused by inflammation of the facial nerve (cranial nerve VII). Alarming to experience, but the great majority of people recover fully.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Start prescribed corticosteroids early to reduce nerve inflammation.
  • Protect the affected eye: lubricating drops by day, taping or a patch at night.
  • Perform facial-muscle exercises and gentle massage (physiotherapy).
  • Use warm compresses to ease discomfort.

Timeline: improvement usually within 2–3 weeks; full recovery in 3–6 months.

Concussion (Mild TBI)

Reversible

A mild traumatic brain injury from a blow or jolt that temporarily disrupts brain function — headache, dizziness, and confusion. With proper rest, symptoms are reversible.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Physical and cognitive rest for the first 24–48 hours.
  • Limit screens, bright lights, and demanding mental tasks.
  • Prioritize sleep and stay hydrated.
  • Follow a gradual, step-by-step return to activity and sport — only while symptom-free.
  • Seek urgent care if symptoms worsen (repeated vomiting, escalating headache, confusion).

Timeline: most people recover within 1–2 weeks.

Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

Highly treatable

Brief episodes of spinning dizziness triggered by head movements, caused by tiny calcium crystals becoming dislodged inside the inner ear. Harmless and very responsive to treatment.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Canalith repositioning (the Epley maneuver) performed by a clinician — often works in one or two sessions.
  • Brandt–Daroff exercises at home to retrain the balance system.
  • Vestibular rehabilitation therapy for any lingering unsteadiness.
  • Change head position slowly while recovering.

Timeline: often resolves within days to a couple of weeks.

Sciatica (Lumbar Radiculopathy)

Usually self-resolving

Pain radiating along the sciatic nerve down the leg, usually from a compressed or irritated nerve root in the lower back. Most cases settle without surgery.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Stay gently active — avoid prolonged bed rest.
  • Physiotherapy: targeted stretching and core-strengthening exercises.
  • Alternate heat and ice on the lower back.
  • Short-term NSAIDs for pain and inflammation.
  • Improve posture and lifting technique to prevent recurrence.

Timeline: most cases improve within 4–6 weeks.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (Early)

Reversible if caught early

Tingling, numbness, and weakness in the hand from compression of the median nerve at the wrist. Caught early, mild cases are often reversible without surgery.

+ Recovery & Therapy
  • Wear a wrist splint — especially at night — to keep the wrist in a neutral position.
  • Adjust ergonomics for your workstation and repetitive tasks.
  • Do nerve-gliding and tendon-gliding exercises.
  • Take periodic rest breaks and use anti-inflammatory measures.

Timeline: mild cases improve over several weeks with consistent care.

Chapter VI

Neurology on a Deeper Level

The Action Potential

The action potential is a brief (~1 ms) reversal of membrane potential — from resting ~−70 mV to a peak of ~+40 mV — that propagates along the axon without decrement. It follows the all-or-nothing principle: sub-threshold stimuli produce no response, while threshold stimuli always produce a full action potential.

Depolarization

Na⁺ channels open → sodium rushes in → membrane potential rises

Repolarization

Na⁺ channels inactivate, K⁺ channels open → potassium exits → potential falls

Hyperpolarization

Brief undershoot below resting potential before returning to baseline

Neural Plasticity

The nervous system is not hardwired — it rewires itself continuously. Synaptic plasticity (changes in synapse strength) underlies learning and memory. Long-term potentiation (LTP) strengthens synapses; long-term depression (LTD) weakens them.

Neuroplasticity also encompasses structural changes: new dendritic spines form, axons sprout new branches, and in some brain regions (hippocampus, olfactory bulb), new neurons are born even in adulthood. After stroke or injury, the brain can reorganize — a phenomenon called cortical remapping.

Clinical Neurology

When the nervous system fails, the consequences are profound. Key areas of neurological disease:

  • Neurodegenerative — Alzheimer's (dementia), Parkinson's (movement), ALS (motor neurons), Huntington's (genetic)
  • Demyelinating — Multiple sclerosis (immune attack on myelin), Guillain-Barré syndrome (PNS demyelination)
  • Vascular — Stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic), transient ischemic attacks
  • Epileptic — Abnormal synchronized firing causing seizures
  • Infectious — Meningitis, encephalitis, neurosyphilis, prion diseases
  • Neuropathic pain — Chronic pain from nerve damage, often involving glial dysfunction

The Connectome & Future Frontiers

Modern neurology is pushing toward mapping the complete wiring diagram of the brain — the connectome. Projects like the Human Connectome Project aim to chart every neural connection, revealing how structure gives rise to function.

Emerging frontiers include optogenetics (controlling neurons with light), brain-computer interfaces (translating neural activity into machine commands), single-cell RNA sequencing to catalog every cell type in the brain, and glymphatic research revealing how the brain clears waste during sleep. The convergence of neuroscience, genetics, AI, and engineering promises a new era of understanding — and treating — the most complex object in the known universe: the human brain.
